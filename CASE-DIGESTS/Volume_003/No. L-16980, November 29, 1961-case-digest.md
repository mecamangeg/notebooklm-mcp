I. CAPTION
IN THE MATTER OF THE PETITION OF ARSENIO G. PE TO BE ADMITTED A CITIZEN OF THE PHILIPPINES, ARSENIO G. PE v. REPUBLIC OF THE PHILIPPINES, G.R. No. L-16980, November 29, 1961, Reyes, J.B.L., J.

II. FACTS
Arsenio G. Pe filed a petition for naturalization with the Court of First Instance of Antique. During the proceedings, his own character witness testified that Pe owned a public utility passenger truck. The witness later amended his testimony, claiming the truck belonged to Peâ€™s father and Pe only managed it. Pe denied both versions, asserting the truck belonged to a third party. The trial court found that the truck was owned by either Pe or his father and noted Pe's admission that he knew alien ownership of a public utility violated the Public Service Act. The trial court denied the petition. Pe appealed, arguing the court erred in its findings and that the government did not file a written opposition.

III. ISSUE/S
W/N the petitioner conducted himself in a proper and irreproachable manner to qualify for naturalization.

IV. RULING
**NO.** The Supreme Court affirmed the denial of the petition. The Court held that the petitioner's ownership and operation of a public utility vehicle, being an alien, is a violation of the Public Service Act [1, 2]. This violation constitutes evidence that he has not conducted himself in a proper and irreproachable manner in his relations with the constituted government as well as with the community in which he is living, which is a requirement under Section 2 of Commonwealth Act No. 473 [3].

The Court emphasized that in naturalization cases, the burden is on the applicant to prove by competent evidence that he has all qualifications and no disqualifications; in case of doubt, the law is construed strictly against the applicant [4]. Additionally, the Court found Pe failed to prove he had a lucrative trade or profession, as his income declarations were dubious and unsupported by tax returns from years prior to his application [5]. Finally, the Court ruled it may *motu proprio* deny an application if the evidence fails to prove requirements have been met, even without written opposition from the government [4].

The judgment appealed from is **AFFIRMED** [6].
