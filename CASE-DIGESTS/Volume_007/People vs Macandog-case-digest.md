I. CAPTION
PEOPLE OF THE PHILIPPINES v. LUALHATI S. MACANDOG, G.R. Nos. L-18601-02, January 31, 1963, Reyes, J.B.L., J.

II. FACTS
On November 25, 1960, the City Fiscalâ€™s office filed two informations against Lualhati S. Macandog in the Municipal Court of Manila for slander and slight physical injuries [1]. Macandog filed motions to quash both informations, arguing that the Municipal Court lacked jurisdiction over the slander case due to the absence of a written complaint by the offended party, and that the slight physical injuries case had prescribed [2]. The Municipal Court denied the motions for lack of merit [2]. Macandog appealed this denial to the Court of First Instance (CFI) of Manila [2]. The CFI dismissed the appeal on the ground that the order denying the motion to quash was interlocutory in nature [1]. Macandog then appealed to the Supreme Court on questions of law [1].

III. ISSUE/S
W/N the order of the lower court denying a motion to quash is appealable. [2]

IV. RULING
**NO.** The Supreme Court affirmed the dismissal of the appeal, holding that an order denying a motion to quash is merely interlocutory and, therefore, not appealable [3, 4].

The Court relied on established jurisprudence (*Collins v. Wolfe*, *People v. Aragon*) and Section 1, Rule 113 of the Rules of Court (now Rule 117), which mandates that after a motion to quash is overruled, the accused "shall immediately plead" [4]. This implies that the trial must proceed, and if a judgment of conviction is rendered, the accused may then appeal and raise the same questions sought to be reviewed in the motion to quash [4].

The Court also clarified that the appellant's reliance on *Arches v. Beldia* was misplaced. The phrase "appeal in due time" in that case refers to an appeal following a conviction after a trial on the merits, not an immediate appeal from an interlocutory order denying a motion to quash [5].

The order appealed from is affirmed [6].
