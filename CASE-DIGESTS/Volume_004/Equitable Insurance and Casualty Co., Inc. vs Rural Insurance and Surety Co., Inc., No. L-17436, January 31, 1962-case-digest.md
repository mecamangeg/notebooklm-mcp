I. CAPTION
EQUITABLE INSURANCE AND CASUALTY COMPANY, INC. v. RURAL INSURANCE AND SURETY COMPANY, INC., G.R. No. L-17436, January 31, 1962, Barrera, J.

II. FACTS
On November 11, 1957, Equitable Insurance (plaintiff) and Rural Insurance (defendant) entered into a reciprocal facultative reinsurance agreement. Pursuant to this agreement, Equitable ceded portions of fire insurance risks to Rural regarding stocks owned by Jaen Bermersâ€™ Cooperative Marketing Association and Electric and Lamp Supplies [1]. Both stocks were subsequently burned. Equitable paid the insured parties and sought reimbursement from Rural for its share of the losses, totaling P2,024.87 and P1,334.80, respectively [1, 2]. Rural refused to pay.

Equitable filed a complaint for collection of sum of money. Rural moved to dismiss, arguing that under Article VIII of their agreement, the dispute must first be submitted to arbitration [2, 3]. The trial court denied the motion. The parties subsequently submitted the case for decision based on a stipulation of facts, wherein Rural admitted all allegations in the complaint, including the amounts due, but insisted on the legal defense regarding arbitration and the nature of the obligation [4, 5]. The trial court ruled in favor of Equitable. Rural appealed to the Court of Appeals, which certified the case to the Supreme Court as it involved purely questions of law [6, 7].

III. ISSUE/S
1. W/N the action is premature due to the failure to submit the matter to arbitration.
2. W/N the term "facultative" in the reinsurance agreement entitles Rural to alternative remedies under Article 1206 of the Civil Code.

IV. RULING
1. **NO.** The arbitration clause in the Reinsurance Agreement applies only when there is a dispute regarding the meaning of the agreement or the liability of the parties [8]. In the stipulation of facts, Rural admitted the existence of the reinsurance, the occurrence of the fires, its liability as reinsurer, and the correctness of the amounts due [9]. Since there was absolutely no dispute regarding the facts or the amount of liability, there was no necessity to submit the matter to arbitrators or an umpire [9]. Furthermore, the Court cited *Chang v. Assurance Corporation*, noting that a refusal to pay may be deemed a waiver of the arbitration condition precedent [10].

2. **NO.** There is no connection between the reinsurance agreement and Article 1206 of the Civil Code regarding facultative obligations (which allow a debtor to substitute a prestation) [11]. In reinsurance contracts, the term "facultative" merely defines the reinsurer's right to accept or reject participation in a risk [11]. Once the risk is accepted, the obligation becomes absolute and can be discharged in only one way: payment of the share of the losses [11]. There is no alternative or substitute prestation available to the reinsurer [11].

The judgment of the trial court is affirmed [12].
