I. CAPTION
VICTORIO GUY CO CHIA v. REPUBLIC OF THE PHILIPPINES, G.R. No. L-17917, April 30, 1964, Bautista Angelo, J.

II. FACTS
Petitioner Victorio Guy Co Chia, born in the Philippines in 1924 to Chinese parents, filed a petition for naturalization in 1959. He did not file a declaration of intention, claiming exemption based on his birth and alleged continuous residence in the Philippines. The Court of First Instance of Manila granted his petition. The Republic appealed, arguing the petitioner failed to file the mandatory declaration of intention and did not qualify for the exemptions under the Naturalization Law. Evidence showed that while the petitioner claimed residence from birth, he was absent from the Philippines and lived in China for 11 years (from 1927 to 1938), in addition to other trips abroad. Furthermore, no evidence was presented to prove he received his primary and secondary education in Philippine public or recognized private schools.

III. ISSUE/S
W/N the petitioner is exempt from filing a declaration of intention to become a Filipino citizen.

IV. RULING
**NO.** The petitioner is not exempt. The Naturalization Law exempts an applicant from filing a declaration of intention only if: (1) they are born in the Philippines and received primary and secondary education in public or recognized schools not limited to any race or nationality; or (2) they have resided continuously in the Philippines for 30 years or more.

Regarding the first ground, the petitioner failed to present evidence that he received the requisite education in recognized schools [1]. Regarding the second ground, the Court held that the petitioner's stay in China from 1927 to 1938 disrupted the 30-year continuous residence requirement. Citing *Dy v. Republic*, the Court ruled that the law contemplates actual and substantial residence, not merely legal residence. An uninterrupted absence of 11 years breaks the continuity required by law, rendering the petitioner unfit to apply for citizenship without the requisite declaration of intention [2].

The decision appealed from is **REVERSED** [3].
