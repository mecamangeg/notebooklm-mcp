I. CAPTION
BELEN CRUZ v. EXEQUIEL CASTILLO, G.R. No. L-27232, June 30, 1969, Capistrano, J.

II. FACTS
Plaintiff Belen Cruz filed a complaint against defendant Exequiel Castillo for compulsory recognition and support of her child, Anselma Armi Castillo [1]. The evidence established that Cruz and Castillo had sexual relations in 1959, resulting in the birth of Anselma on July 11, 1960 [2]. Castillo initially denied paternity and liability for support [3]. The trial court ruled in favor of Cruz, declaring Anselma as Castilloâ€™s natural child and ordering him to pay monthly support calculated from the date of the filing of the complaint [4]. Castillo appealed to the Court of Appeals, which certified the case to the Supreme Court as it involved purely questions of law [5]. On appeal, Castillo admitted paternity but argued that the lower court erred in ordering support to commence from the filing of the complaint rather than the date of the judgment [5].

III. ISSUE/S
W/N support for a natural child who has not been voluntarily recognized should commence from the date of the filing of the complaint.

IV. RULING
**NO.** The Court ruled that the obligation to support commences only from the date of the judgment compelling recognition. Under the Civil Code, a natural child not recognized by the father has no rights, not even to support [5]. It is the fact of recognition, whether voluntary (under Article 278) or compulsory (under Article 283), that confers the rights of support and succession upon the child [6]. Since the defendant did not voluntarily recognize the child, his obligation to support did not exist until the trial court rendered judgment declaring him the father and compelling recognition [6]. Consequently, the support should commence from July 27, 1964, the date of the lower court's judgment [7]. The Supreme Court modified the decision, ordering the defendant to recognize the child and increasing the support to P100.00 per month starting from the date of the trial court's judgment [7].
