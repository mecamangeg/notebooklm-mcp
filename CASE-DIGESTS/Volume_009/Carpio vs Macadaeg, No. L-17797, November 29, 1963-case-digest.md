**I. CAPTION**
ISABELO CARPIO v. HON. HIGINIO MACADAEG and OSCAR C. ABAYA, G.R. No. L-17797, November 29, 1963, Makalintal, J. [1, 2]

**II. FACTS**
Respondent Oscar Abaya filed a complaint against Petitioner Isabelo Carpio for the recovery of money. Before summons was served, Abaya filed an *ex parte* motion for preliminary attachment, alleging that Carpio was about to dispose of his property to defraud creditors [3, 4]. Respondent Judge Macadaeg issued the writ, resulting in the garnishment of Carpio's imported goods and the seizure of five race horses [3].

Carpio moved to discharge the attachment, denying the fraud allegations. The Judge initially set aside the attachment orders but later reversed himself and reinstated the attachment upon Abayaâ€™s motion for reconsideration, which cited Carpio's alleged sale of horses and furniture as proof of fraud [5, 6]. Carpio controverted these allegations, but the Judge reinstated the attachment without conducting a hearing to verify the claim of fraud [6, 7]. The Judge subsequently ordered the sale of the attached horses and goods, prompting Carpio to file a petition for certiorari and prohibition with the Supreme Court [2, 8].

**III. ISSUE/S**
W/N the respondent Judge acted with grave abuse of discretion in issuing the writ of preliminary attachment. [9]

**IV. RULING**
**YES.** The Court ruled that the issuance of the writ was improper and declared the orders null and void [10].

First, the mere removal or disposal of property is not a sufficient ground for the issuance of preliminary attachment; pursuant to Sec. 1(e), Rule 59 of the Rules of Court, such removal or disposal must be made with the specific intent to defraud creditors [4].

Second, when the allegation of fraudulent disposal was controverted by the petitioner, the Judge should have granted the parties an opportunity to prove their respective claims in a hearing before issuing the writ [7]. Citing *National Coconut Corporation v. Pecson* and *Villongco v. Panlilio*, the Court emphasized that the petitioner should have been given the chance to show that he was not disposing of his property in fraud of creditors [7, 11].

Third, the affidavit submitted by Abaya in support of his motion was insufficient under Sec. 3, Rule 59 of the Rules of Court, as it failed to establish that a sufficient cause of action existed or that the case was one of those where attachment is allowed [12, 13].

**Petition GRANTED.** The orders of the respondent Judge were declared null and void, the attached properties were ordered released, and the preliminary injunction issued by the Supreme Court was made permanent [10].
