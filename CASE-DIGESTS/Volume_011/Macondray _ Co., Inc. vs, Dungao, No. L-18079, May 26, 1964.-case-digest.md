I. CAPTION
MACONDRAY & CO., INC. v. BERNARDO S. DUNGAO and RIZAL SURETY & INSURANCE CO., INC., G.R. No. L-18079, May 26, 1964, Padilla, J.

II. FACTS
Bernardo S. Dungao executed a promissory note for P6,100.00 in favor of Macondray & Co., Inc. secured by a mortgage on personal property [1, 2]. When Dungao defaulted, Macondray & Co. filed an action for the collection of the sum of money in the Court of First Instance (CFI) of Manila on July 1, 1949 [3]. A writ of attachment was issued against Dungao's properties but was subsequently dissolved on September 21, 1949, after Dungao and Rizal Surety & Insurance Co., Inc. filed a surety bond [3].

On July 30, 1952, before the trial concluded, Dungao died [4]. His counsel moved for dismissal, arguing the action for money recovery did not survive his death [4]. The CFI denied the motion, ordered the substitution of the administratrix of Dungaoâ€™s estate, and rendered judgment ordering the estate and the surety to pay the principal amount plus interest and attorney's fees [5, 6]. The defendants appealed, questioning the jurisdiction of the trial court after the defendant's death [7].

III. ISSUE/S
W/N the action for collection of money survives the death of the defendant pending final judgment in the Court of First Instance.

IV. RULING
**NO.** The Supreme Court held that the action for recovery of money, debt, or interest does not survive the death of the defendant. Under Section 21 of Rule 3 of the Rules of Court, such an action must be dismissed to be prosecuted in the manner provided for the settlement of estates [8, 9]. Although the promissory note stated it was secured by a mortgage, the plaintiff did not seek foreclosure but purely the collection of a sum of money [10].

While the levy on attachment might have constituted an exception to the general rule on non-survival of claims, the attachment was discharged upon the filing of a bond, causing the property to revert to its previous condition free from specific liens [9, 10]. The rationale is to ensure all creditors receive a proportionate share of the estate [9]. However, the surety bond filed to discharge the attachment subsists and continues to answer for any money claim the plaintiff may be allowed by the probate court [11].

The judgments and order appealed from were **SET ASIDE**, without prejudice to the filing of the money claim by the plaintiff with the probate court [12].
